Omnidox

Understand Understanding

When we are talking about understanding a subject, it’s important to recognize that understanding has different levels and different aspects.

One way to gauge one person’s understanding is to see what questions about the subject can he/she answer. Take React flux for example: what are the signatures of its APIs? What problem is it trying to solve? What characteristics of React/Web determine its necessity? Who invented it? What is its pros & cons? Who are improving it, and in what direction? We will judge a person who can answer all these questions as having deep understanding, but for those who can only answer a few, we also think they somewhat understand the subject, just to a shallower level.

Another way is to measure one’s capability to manipulate or predict properties/states/behavior of the subject. This line focuses on our goals of understanding. Unfortunately, this angle also tells us that it’s almost impossible to have perfect understanding, because our science is nowhere close to predicting the chaotic system. (A very good lecture on this by a Stanford biology professor)

These two ways of evaluation complement each other: to judge whether the person have a good answer, we often need to think if the answer can help us do something and how much. E.g. for the question ‘What problem is it trying to solve?’, if one answers merely the concrete historical event (in the case of React flux, it’s to prevent accidently updating other views when one view changes) while doesn’t explicitly state the generalizable idea behind it (to keep the data flow in web applications unidirectional so that the program can be easier to reason about), we won’t think he/she deeply understands it. Conversely, we don’t consider people who can dispatch tasks but not able to explain the rationale/science as experts, because their capability is limited and brittle.
